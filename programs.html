Program Details


Media Career Advancement Program



MCAP is year-round program. Activities are conducted throughout the school year and over the summer months. Typically, the activity begins in the fall of each calendar year. The components of this (12) month cycle are as follows:
  • September - December ( recruitment, curriculum design)
  • January - March (high school lectures and student recruitment applications)
  • April - May (planning and program preparation)
  • June - August (summer internship training, lectures, college field trips)
  • Annual Gala and Scholarships Awards Presentation.


What are the key components of MCAP?



MCAP Foundation has five integrated and inter-dependent components that are essential to the success of each MCAP participant. These are Instruction, Internship, Mentoring, Continued Education, and Community Service.


How does the MCAP Instruction component work?



Hands-on-Training. This hands-on training approach, although typical for industry, is generally not available in high school environments. Our focus is to develop real-life scenario skills that participants will use in job situations. Each student works in a real broadcast studio performing tasks they will perform in job settings upon completion of the program (either in college or the workplace).
MCAP professional instructors teach in-depth techniques and procedures for audio and video, including working with state-of-the-art digital broadcast television equipment. Students work with next generation non-linear production suites and receive extensive training with cameras, switchers, video and audio editors, digital video effects equipment, computer graphics, virtual sets, 3D effects, chroma-key, time base correctors, reading wave form and vector scopes, lighting and teleprompters, TV and radio operations and engineering, production techniques and technical troubleshooting.

Technical Instructors. MCAP has some of the most talented professional video and sound instructors from throughout Indiana’s major stations and production facilities in central Indiana. Many are Emmy and other industry award winning professionals with decades of technical and managerial experience in national and local settings. Key state-of-the-art technical classes include, but are not limited to: Broadcast Television and Radio Systems, Digital Video Editing, Video Production, Web-Site Development, Voice & Data Telecommunications, Computer Graphics, Executive Production, Studio/Field Cameras, Broadcast Operations, Publications & E-Publications, Computer Engineering, and Production Graphic Design.

Skills Training. Technical skill training is a given. However, life skill training is also necessary in order to compete and have a successful career. This training is generally not offered in high schools, colleges, and universities. This includes, self-esteem building, effective communication, problem solving, conflict resolution, relationship building, resume design, and interviewing.


How does the MCAP Internship component work?



MCAP creates summer jobs (paid internships) for its youth and young adults participants. The MCAP program activities prepare participants to overcome many of the obstacles encountered when seeking suitable employment. MCAP’s program experiences allow students to become more competitive in the market for summer and full time opportunities. Much of the instruction will prepare them with skill levels comparable with college students who have taken coursework specific to their major.

Unlike traditional intern programs, MCAP students work for partner companies during summers and school breaks from the summer following sophomore year through the summer after graduation. During the internship program, students have an opportunity to learn many aspects of corporate operations and develop company-specific skills. Corporations have an opportunity to train and mentor students with the option of fulltime employment.

Once selected for an internship by corporate sponsors, MCAP proactively monitors the progress and training details of the students. MCAP monitors each student’s progress, attendance skills and work performance throughout their internship. MCAP staff acts in an advisory capacity for both interns and sponsoring companies, especially if questions or problems arise.


How does the MCAP Mentoring component work?



Mentoring is an important component that augments both the instruction and internship provided to each student. One of the key variables to a successful career is a support team to help guide each student’s development. Due to their age and high learning curve, MCAP matches students with media professionals currently employed in the industry.

Each student is assigned both a mentor at their place(s) of internship as well as other broadcast experts who volunteer to help with the mentoring process. Mentoring plans are developed for each MCAP student.


How does the MCAP Continuing Education component work?



MCAP provides continuing education class modules for the students through direct instruction, webinars, online classes, and workshops. MCAP staff tracks and monitors students' skills and work performance throughout the program and tracks graduates' career progress. The MCAP staff will collaborate to develop and implement detailed career development plans that outline the steps for the student’s success in the broadcasting and media industry.

MCAP offers students a lifetime experience. MCAP tracks each program participant well after high school graduation. MCAP continuously monitors students' skills and work performance throughout the program and tracks graduates' career progress. MCAP staff continues to work with students to develop and implement detailed career development plans that outline the steps for their success in the broadcasting and media industry.


How are students recruited and selected to participate in MCAP?



Selection of participants is a tedious and competitive process. MCAP endeavors to recruit and select from a diverse pool of students. Special considerations for students from underserved and/or underrepresented communities may also be made.

MCAP works with the directors of many school systems’ technology centers and programs to identify and recruit students who meet our criteria for participation. Referrals and recommendations are obtained from teachers, counselors and administrators. Completion of an essay is required. An interview and parental permission are mandatory.

MCAP evaluates a number of key characteristics and abilities for each student, including:

  • Math and science aptitude
  • Grade Point Average
  • Career aspirations
  • Communications and writing skills
  • Life skills
  • Math and science aptitude
  • Leadership skills, and ability to work within team environment.


How does MCAP measure success?



Each major component of MCAP has specific criteria for evaluating success, including grades in courses, mentor and sponsor feedback, equipment operation skill demonstrations, and community service related performance. Moreover, upon completion of the program, our graduates must have developed skills that are commensurate with current professional practices and standards, thus making them truly competitive and a desired hire.

Our program tracks the number of created opportunities for a multitude of young people who had not been able to gain access to employment. MCAP students are evaluated for their demonstrated ability to work in a professional environment under supervision, to successfully interact with other employees in a team environment, and to contribute to the overall mission of the sponsoring organization. MCAP also measures its success by evaluating the effectiveness by which it develops and builds specific skills and capabilities prior to students selecting and entering a college career path.
